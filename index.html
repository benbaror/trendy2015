Hill's Problem: Context

  • The three body problem: Moon, Earth and Sun
  • Applicable to a nearly circular orbit
  • Has many applications: (Solar system, Star clusters, binary disruption around a massive black hole, etc.)

Applications for the escape from a star cluster

  • Due to the tidal forces stars gradually escape from the cluster
  • Expected on theoretical grounds and confirmed by observations and simulations
  • The energy of stars changes by two-body encounters
  • If the stellar energy exceeds some critical energy the star can escape

Cluster on a circular orbit

  • Rotation: \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}=\left(\Omega,0,0\right)\)
  • Velocity: \(\dot{\mathbf{x}}=\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\left(\mathbf{R}+\mathbf{r}\right)+\dot{\mathbf{r}}\),
  • Acceleration: \(\ddot{\mathbf{x}}=-\nabla\Phi_{g}\left(\mathbf{R}+\mathbf{r}\right)-\nabla\Phi_{c}\left(\mathbf{r}\right)\) \(=\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\left(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\left(\mathbf{R}+\mathbf{r}\right)\right)+2\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}+\ddot{\mathbf{r}}\)
  • E.O.M in the rotating frame: Centrifugal, Coriolis and gravitational forces

\(\ddot{\mathbf{r}}=\color{cyan}{-\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\left(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\left(\mathbf{R}+\mathbf{r}\right)\right)}\color{green}{-2\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}}-\nabla\Phi_{g}\left(\mathbf{R}+\mathbf{r}\right)-\nabla\Phi_{c}\left(\mathbf{r}\right)\)

The tidal approximation

  • Assuming \(r \ll R\) and a spherical symmetric potential: \(\nabla\Phi_{g}\left(\mathbf{r}+\mathbf{R}\right) = \Phi_{g}^{\prime}\left(R\right)\hat{R}+\left(\mathbf{r}\cdot\nabla\right)\Phi_{g}^{\prime}\left(\mathbf{R}\right)\hat{R}\)
  • Using \(d^{2}\mathbf{R}/dt^{2}=\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\left(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{R}\right)=-\Phi^{\prime}\left(R\right)\hat{R}=-\Omega^{2}\mathbf{R}\) we have \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\left(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{R}\right)+\nabla\Phi_{g}\left(\mathbf{r}+\mathbf{R}\right)=2\Omega\Omega^{\prime}\left(\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{R}\right)\hat{R}+\Omega^{2}\mathbf{r}\)
  • Equations of motion \(\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -2\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}-\nabla\Phi_{eff}\left(\mathbf{r}\right)\)
  • Effective Potential \(\Phi_{eff}\left(\mathbf{r}\right)=\frac{1}{2}\Omega^{2}z^{2}+\frac{1}{2}\left(\kappa^{2}-4\Omega^{2}\right)x^{2}+\Phi_{c}\left(\mathbf{r}\right)\) where \(\kappa^{2}=\Phi_{g}^{\prime\prime}\left(R\right)+3\Omega^{2}\)

The tidal radius

  • The energy is an integral of motion (the Jacobi integral) \(E=\frac{1}{2}\dot{r}^{2}+\Phi_{eff}\)
  • Lagrange points: Stationary orbits \(r_{t} = -\Phi_{c}^{\prime}\left(r_{t}\right)/\left(\kappa^{2}-4\Omega^{2}\right)\)
  • The critical energy \(E_{c}=E(r_t)=\frac{3}{2}\Phi_{c}\left(r_{t}\right)\left(1- \left.\frac{1}{3}\frac{d\log r\Phi_{c}(r)}{d\log r} \right|_{r=r_{t}}\right)\)
  • Assuming a Keplerian potential \(\Phi_{c}\left(r\right)=-GM/r\); \(r_{t}^{3}=GM/(4\Omega^{2}-\kappa^{2})\); \(E_{c}=-3GM/2r_{t}\)

Escaping from the cluster

  • None escapers: \(E < E_{c}\)
  • Real escapers: \(E>E_{c}\) and \(x\left(\infty\right)>r_{t}\)
  • Potential escapers: \(E>E_{c}\) but \(x(t) < r_{t}\) for all \(t\)
  • Fake escapers: \(E>E_{c}\) and \(x(t) > r_{t}\) for some \(t\) but \(x(\infty) < r_{t}\)

Escape rate

  • Flux: The phase volume, per unit energy, across \(x=r_{t}\) (Fukushige Heggie 2000) \[\mathcal{F}\left(\epsilon\right)=\int_{\dot{x}>0}\delta\left(E-E\left(\mathbf{r},\dot{\mathbf{r}}\right)\right)\dot{x}d^{3}\dot{\mathbf{r}}dydz\]
  • Volume: phase space volume per unit of energy \[\mathcal{V}\left(\epsilon\right)=\int\delta\left(E-E\left(\mathbf{r},\dot{\mathbf{r}}\right)\right)d^{3}\mathbf{r}d^{3}\dot{\mathbf{r}}\]
  • Escape time \(t_e = \mathcal{V}/\mathcal{F}\)

Circular case: Summary

  • Integral of motion: The Jacobi integral
  • Lagrange points: unstable stationary points
  • Critical energy: Escape criterion

Cluster on eccentric orbit: Motivation

  • Stars escape the cluster due to galactic tides
  • Most GCs are on eccentric orbits
  • Cluster evolution depends on eccentricity
  • No theory for the escape rate

Adopted from Baumgardt and Makino (2003)

Cluster on eccentric orbit: Challenges

  • No integral of motion
  • No stationary orbits
  • Phase dependent critical energy
  • Phase dependent flux

Outline of the Analytical approach

  • Generalized Equations of motion for a cluster on an elliptic orbit
  • Unstable periodic orbits: generalization of the Lagrange points
  • Three-body toy model: star, cluster, galaxy
  • Linearized equations of motion near the “Lagrange points”
  • Escape volume over one orbital time

Cluster on an elliptic orbit

  • Rotation: \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}=\left(\Omega(t),0,0\right)\)
  • Velocity: \(\dot{\mathbf{x}}=\dot{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathrm{circ}}+\dot{\mathbf{R}}\)
  • Acceleration: \(\ddot{\mathbf{x}}=\ddot{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathrm{circ}} + \dot{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}\times\left(\mathbf{R}+\mathbf{r}\right) + \ddot{\mathbf{R}} + 2\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\dot{\mathbf{R}}\)
  • E.O.M in the rotating frame:

\(\ddot{\mathbf{r}}=\ddot{\mathbf{r}}_{\mathrm{circ}} - \boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\dot{\mathbf{R}} - \dot{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}\times\left(\mathbf{R}+\mathbf{r}\right) - \ddot{\mathbf{R}}\)

  • Tidal approximation (\(r\ll R\)): \(\ddot{\mathbf{r}}=\ddot{\mathbf{r}}_{\mathrm{circ}}+2\frac{\dot{R}}{R}\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r}+\frac{\ddot{R}}{R}\left(\mathbf{r}-x\hat{R}\right)\)

Lagrange pointsorbits

  • Generalized tidal radius: Periodic orbits of the form \(\mathbf{r}=\left(\pm r_{t}\left(t\right),0,0\right)\)

    \begin{eqnarray*} \ddot{x}&=&-r_t\left(\Phi_g^{\prime\prime}(R)-\Omega^{2}\right)-\Phi_c^\prime(r_t)\\\ddot{y}&=&2\frac{\Omega}{R}\left(\dot{R}r_t-R\dot{r}_t\right)=0 \end{eqnarray*}
  • Therefore \(r_t=\alpha R\) and \(\ddot{r}_{t}=\alpha\ddot{R}\).
  • A solution exist if \[\Phi_c^\prime(R)/\alpha=\Phi_g^\prime(R)-R\Phi_g^{\prime\prime}(R)\] for constant \(\alpha\)
  • For example point-mass approximation (Hill's Problem):
    \(\Phi_c(r_t)=-GM/r\) and \(\Phi_{g}(R)=-GM_{g}/R\)

Regularization

  • Consider the transformation \(\mathbf{\tilde{r}}=\mathbf{r}/r_t\), \(\tau=\int\Omega dt\)
  • Equations of motion: \[ \tilde{\mathbf{r}}^{\prime\prime}=-\nabla_{\tilde{\mathbf{r}}}\tilde{\Phi}_{eff}(\tilde{\mathbf{r}})-2\hat{z}\times\dot{\tilde{\mathbf{r}}}\\ \]
  • Effective potential \[ \tilde{\Phi}_{eff}=\frac{1}{2}\tilde{z}^2-3(1+e\cos(\phi_0 + \tau))^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{\tilde{r}}+\frac{1}{2}\tilde{x}^2\right) \]
  • Stationary 'Lagrange points' \(\tilde{x}_t=\pm1\)
  • Phase dependent critical energy: \[ \tilde{E}_c = -(9/2)/(1+e\cos(\phi_0+\tau)) \]

Linearization

  • Expansion near the Lagrange point \(q_1 = \tilde{x}-1\), \(q_2 = \tilde{y}\)

  • Quadratic Hamiltonian

    \begin{eqnarray*} \tilde{H} &=& \frac{1}{2}(p_1^2+p_2^2) + p_1q_2 - p_2q_1 \\ && - \frac{1}{2(1-e\cos\phi)}(9q_1^2-3q_2^2) \end{eqnarray*}
  • Stars can escape if \(\tilde{H} > 0\)

Circular Case Eigenvalues: \(\sim\pm2.5, \pm2.07i\)

Elliptical Case

Escape rate:

  • Escape volume per unit of energy: \[ \mathcal{N}(\epsilon,\tau)= \int\Theta[q_1(\tau)] \delta(\epsilon - \tilde{H}(\mathbf{w}))d^4\mathbf{w} \] where \(\mathbf{w} = (q_1, q_2, p_1, p_2)^T\)
  • Time dependent Flux: \(\mathcal{F}(\epsilon, t)=\Omega d{\mathcal{N}}(\epsilon, \tau)/d\tau\)
  • Constant Flux:
    • \(\mathcal{F}_0(\epsilon)=\mathcal{F}(\epsilon, 0)\)
    • \(\bar{\mathcal{F}}(\epsilon)= \mathcal{N}(\epsilon, 2\pi)/2\pi/P\)
  • Escape time: \(t_e = \mathcal{V}(\epsilon)/\bar{\mathcal{F}}(\epsilon)\)
  • Life time (Baumgardt 2001): \(t_d = t_r^{3/4}t_e^{1/4}\)

Elliptic case

  • Hamiltonian (perturbations): \[ \tilde{H}(\phi,e) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{w}^TC_0\mathbf{w} + \frac{1}{2}e\cos\phi\mathbf{w}^TC_1\mathbf{w} + \dots \]
  • Find a canonical linear transformation \(\mathbf{w} = (I + eM_1(\phi)+e^2M_2(\phi))\mathbf{W}\)
  • The Hamiltonian \(\tilde{H}^\prime(e) = \tilde{H}_0 + e\tilde{H}_1 + e^2\tilde{H}_2\) is independent of \(\phi\) (integral of motion)
  • General form: \(M_1 = S_1\cos(\phi) + S_2\sin(\phi)\), \(M_2 = S_3 + S_4\cos(2\phi) + S_5\sin(2\phi)\), where \(JS_i + S_i^TJ = 0\)
  • Escape volume per unit of energy: \[ \mathcal{N}(\epsilon,\tau)= \int\Theta[Q_1(\tau)] \delta(\epsilon - \tilde{H}^\prime(e,\mathbf{W}))d^4\mathbf{W} \]

Elliptic case: 1st order in \(e\)

  • Hamiltonian: \(\tilde{H}^\prime(e) = \tilde{H}_0\) (independent of \(e\))
  • In the new coordinates same dynamics as in the circular case.
  • Conclusion: To first order, independent of eccentricity
  • We must go to next order.

Elliptic case: 2nd order in \(e\)

  • Hamiltonian: \(\tilde{H}^\prime(e) = \tilde{H}_0 + e^2\tilde{H}_2\) (depends on \(e\))
  • Different dynamics
  • Flux: \(\bar{\mathcal{F}}(e) \approx \bar{\mathcal{F}}(0)(1 + 7.4e^2)\)
  • Volume: \(\bar{\mathcal{V}}(e) \approx \bar{\mathcal{V}}(0)(1 + 4e^2)\)
  • Escape time \(t_e(e) \approx t_{e}(0)(1-3.4e^2)\)
  • Life time: \(\color{yellow}{t_d(e) \approx t_d(0)(1-0.86e^2)}\)

Summary

  • To first order in \(e\) the an elliptic orbit can be mapped to a circular one.
  • Life time is independent of \(e\) to first order in \(e\)
  • Life time is of order \(t_r^{3/4} P^{1/4} (1-0.86e^2)\) in agreement with \(N\)-body.
